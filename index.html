Methodology

Pipeline design, scoring scale, model setup, ensemble construction, and how to read the dashboard.

Overview

The CB Sentiment Project measures the hawkish or dovish stance of 23 central banks by scoring official monetary policy statements with a 3-model LLM ensemble. Each statement is split into sentences; each sentence is scored on a continuous −10 (strongly hawkish) to +10 (strongly dovish) scale; the document score is the sentence-level ensemble mean. Sentence-level scoring is a deliberate design choice: a single policy statement routinely mixes inflation concern, growth risk, and forward guidance in different proportions. Document-level scoring collapses this structure; sentence-level scoring preserves it.

Data Coverage

More than 4,500 statements across 23 central banks are scored. Banks were selected on three criteria: market relevance (the currency matters for global FX and fixed-income markets), policy independence (the bank sets rates or financial conditions autonomously, not via a peg or mandate from another authority), and communication accessibility. Together the 23 banks cover currencies representing over 90% of global average daily FX turnover (BIS 2022 Triennial Survey).

The most notable exclusion is the Bank of Japan (JPY, ~17% of FX turnover). The transition from yield curve control to conventional rate targeting in 2024 creates a regime break that makes pre- and post-transition scores incomparable on a single scale. Other exclusions: currency boards (HKMA), exchange-rate targeters (MAS), and banks with no accessible English-language statement archive (PBOC).

Scoring Scale

The scale runs from −10 (strongly hawkish) to +10 (strongly dovish), with 0 representing a fully neutral stance. In practice, empirical scores for most central banks cluster in a narrower range reflecting the measured communication style of monetary policymakers, but the full scale is available to represent extreme episodes.

−10 Strongly hawkish 0 Neutral +10 Strongly dovish

Why −10/+10? Empirical Validation

The scale choice was tested empirically using 307 representative sentences scored by all three models under three alternative scales: −2/+2 (2pt), −10/+10 (current), and −100/+100.

2pt scale: two failure modes. At neutral (score = 0), roughly 43% of sentences are genuinely neutral, 32% are mildly hawkish, and 23% mildly dovish — the label is uninformative. At the extremes (−2, +2), the bucket fans across −5 to −10 and +5 to +10 on the finer scale; a label of "strongly hawkish" collapses a cautious −5 and an emergency −10 into the same bin.

100pt scale: false precision. Models use fewer than 30 of the 201 theoretical levels in practice (~14% utilisation), clustering at multiples of 5 or 10. No incremental information over the 10pt scale; the extra granularity is noise.

10pt scale: correct. Models use 95% of the 21 available levels; cross-model agreement is highest here (median r = 0.915 across pairs); the scale captures both directional ambiguity and intensity without false precision.

Why GPT-4o Was Dropped

All four shortlisted models were run on a stratified sample of 249 central bank statements spanning multiple CBs, languages, and policy regimes. Each model received the same prompt and scored each statement independently. Two statistics determined the outcome:

  • Correlation (Pearson r). Pairs that exclude GPT-4o achieve r = 0.91–0.95. Every pair involving GPT-4o falls to r = 0.73–0.75 — a consistent 0.17–0.20 point drop regardless of which other model it is paired with. This is not noise: it is a structural signal that GPT-4o is reading something different in the statements.
  • Systematic bias. GPT-4o scores approximately +1.0 units higher (more dovish) than the consensus on every pair. The non-GPT pairs have bias indistinguishable from zero (0.02–0.10). Including a systematically biased model in an equal-weight average would shift every ensemble score by ~0.3 units — a meaningful distortion across 4,500+ statements.

Each model receives the same structured prompt asking it to assess whether a sentence signals hawkish or dovish monetary policy intent. The prompt requires a numeric score and a brief textual justification — forcing the model to reason through the sentence before committing to a number, which measurably reduces score randomness compared to prompts that ask for numbers alone. All 23 central banks use an identical prompt framing, ensuring cross-CB comparability regardless of communication style or language register.

Ensemble Construction

Aggregation is bottom-up, operating at the sentence level. Each model scores each sentence independently with no cross-model communication. Sentences from different models are matched by normalized text — identical surface strings across models are treated as the same sentence. For each matched sentence, the ensemble score is the mean of the non-null model scores for that sentence. The document score is then the mean of all sentence ensemble scores.

This structure preserves the information content that flat document-level averaging discards. A statement mixing hawkish inflation language with dovish growth hedges produces a document score that reflects the composition of those signals sentence-by-sentence, not just their net sum. Cross-model agreement at the sentence level also surfaces where the models disagree — a higher-entropy signal that individual scoring conceals.

When a model fails to score a sentence (rare), the sentence ensemble uses the remaining models. Statements where all models fail or return insufficient output are excluded.

Text Processing

Statements are scraped directly from official central bank websites. Pre-processing steps:

  1. Remove boilerplate (headers, footers, legal disclaimers, contact info)
  2. Split into sentences using standard tokenization
  3. Score each sentence independently; the model prompt instructs each model to return null for non-substantive content (dates, citations, procedural headers, legal boilerplate) rather than forcing a score

PDF documents (older statements) are extracted with column-aware word ordering to handle two-column press release layouts.

Aggregation & Smoothing

Raw document scores are resampled to monthly end-of-month frequency. Group and aggregate index charts apply a 3-month rolling average to reduce meeting-cycle noise without losing cycle-turning-point responsiveness. Individual CB charts display raw meeting scores as dots, with a 3-month rolling average overlaid as a line.

Momentum Indicator

The momentum indicator measures the speed and direction of change in each CB's stance — not where sentiment is, but how quickly and in which direction it is moving. It is computed on normalized, meeting-to-meeting score changes over several overlapping lookback windows, then capped on a −2 (hawkish) to +2 (dovish) scale. The multi-window construction with per-CB normalization makes the indicator scale-invariant: a momentum reading of +1.0 means the same thing for a historically volatile CB as for a historically stable one. The half-life of the indicator is approximately 6 months.

Observations

Key empirical findings — factor structure and regional behaviour.

1. PC1 Captures the Global Policy Cycle

Principal component analysis reveals a dominant first component (PC1) capturing the synchronized global monetary policy cycle — most visibly the 2021–2023 tightening that swept DM and EM simultaneously. PC1 shows positive loadings on all constituents and is generally not dominated by a few.

PC1 tracks the equal-weight index closely for any group, confirming the EW average captures the dominant factor with minimal information loss. The same structure holds for the momentum signal.

2. Regional Patterns & Divergence

DM banks are more tightly synchronized than EM. Within DM, NA and Europe move in near-lockstep during policy cycles — 5-year pairwise correlations run 0.7–0.9. LATAM banks (BRL, CLP, COP, MXN) led the 2021 tightening by roughly two quarters ahead of the FED and ECB, reacting earlier to commodity and currency-driven inflation. Several EMEA banks (CZK, HUF, PLN) followed a similar early-mover pattern.
